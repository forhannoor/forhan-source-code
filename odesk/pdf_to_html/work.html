About APOE Status

I use the Promethease software to identify APOE genotype.

APOE stands for "apolipoprotein E" and is involved in lipoprotein-, cholesterol- and fat soluble vitamin transport.

APOE is also believed to transport heavy metals (including mercury) out of the brain. The latter function may be the link between Alzheimer's disease and its association with mercury- and aluminum exposure and the accumulation of these and other heavy metals in the body.

There are essentially three basic variants of the APOE protein, and each one is associated with more or less risk for acquiring heart disease and Alzheimer's (possibly other diseases, such as Parkinson's, as well), and also (but more speculatively) to APOE's variable capacity for eliminating toxic metals depending on which variants one has.

The APOE variants are:

APOE 2: "better"
APOE 3: "normal"
APOE 4: "bad"

Each person has a combination of two of the above, so a person can have for example APOE 4:4 or APOE 3:4 or APOE 3:3, APOE 2:3, APOE 2:4, and so on.

Combinations with a "4" are often flagged as a risk factor for Alzheimer's disease, and APOE 4:4 is considered a risk factor for "early onset" of the disease.

About Methylation

"Methylation" is the broad label for biochemical processes involving enzymes "tagging and untagging" bioactive molecules with "methyl groups" (CH3).

As these processes are both ubiquitous in the body and governed by specific genes in which common variants/mutations are common, methylation is one of the most important areas where nutrition can interact with one's genetic makeup in a powerful but non-obvious way. This makes genetic testing a very helpful tool.

Methylation concerns countless processes in the body including neurotransmitter production/balance, the detoxification and elimination of environmental- and internally generated toxins, the turning on and off of genes ("epigenetics" or "gene expression"), and immune function.

Problems with methylation has been tied to psychiatric diseases and syndromes such as Autism, Schizophrenia and others, but can also affect a more healthy person's ability to concentrate on tasks and to experience appropriate emotions.

Methylation problems are also tied to many types of cancer because of methylation's role in regulating gene expression as well as protecting the body from toxicity from carcinogens.

COMT V158M (+/-), COMT H62H (+/-) VDR taq (-/-)

Catechol–O–Methyl Transferase (COMT) degrades dopamine, norepinephrine, and to a lesser extent other neurotransmitter substances. John has two single copies (heterozygous) mutations that down-regulate COMT and therefore may result in higher dopamine levels.

When COMT down-regulation is present, too many methyl groups such as from methylcobalamin (methyl-B12), 5-methyltetrahydrofolate (5-MTHF), S-adenosylmethionine (SAMe), and trimethylglycine (TMG) can lead to mood swings or feeling wired or stressed up.

Overall, John should be careful and titrate slowly if she ever takes “methyl-donor” supplements such as some liver- or detox- supplements including the specific items mentioned above. Note that she also lacks most mutations (e.g., MTHFR) that would increase her need for such supplements.

Tiana’s form of vitamin D receptor (VDR taq) may somewhat intensify the effects of her COMT mutations.


BHMT 2(+/+) , BHMT 4(+/+), BHMT 8 (+/+)

You have three double (or "homozygous”) mutations in the gene that codes for Betaine-Homocysteine Methyltransferase (BHMT). These mutations may limit the conversion of homocysteine back into methionine through a "short cut" that is not dependent on folate and B12.

For this reason, BHMT may cause homocysteine to more easily convert into toxic sulfur compounds and ammonia. Ammonia detoxification depends on a molecule called BH4 which may impact your levels of neurotransmitters (discussed below).

BHMT mutations may impact levels of norepinephrine in a way that has been tied to ADHD.

If John experiences problems that could be traced to BHMT, phosphatidylcholine or phosphatidylserine may be used to stimulate BHMT for better function.



MAO A R297R (+/-)

John has two copies of a mutation in the gene that codes for Monoamine Oxidase A which causes slower breakdown of serotonin. This is a very common variant, and perhaps not very significant, but in some individuals it could result in mood swings.
